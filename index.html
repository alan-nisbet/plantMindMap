Plants — A Living Reference
🪴 Indoor Plants 6 topics ▶
Most people place indoor plants by best guess — an empty corner, a bare shelf — without considering what that room actually offers. Every room in a home has a distinct light and humidity signature: bathrooms and kitchens run humid, living rooms and bedrooms run dry and bright or dim depending on exposure, hallways and windowless offices run dim year-round. Match the plant's native habitat to the room's actual conditions and most "I can't keep anything alive" problems disappear. Each topic below includes a Where to Put It section with specific room guidance.
Foliage Houseplants ▶
  • Pothos
  • Monstera deliciosa
  • Snake Plant
  • ZZ Plant
  • Peace Lily
  • Chinese Evergreen
  • Rubber Tree
  • Bird of Paradise
Where to Put Them
🛁 Best: Bathroom (if it has a window)
Showers and baths push humidity well above the 40–60% range these plants evolved with in tropical forest understories. A bright bathroom is one of the best rooms in the house for Monstera, Pothos, and similar foliage — better than a dry, sunny living room.
🍳 Also Good: Kitchen
Cooking steam plus a sink and dishwasher running daily keep ambient moisture higher than most other rooms. Cluster two or three pots together near the counter — grouped foliage raises local humidity further by trapping each plant's own transpired moisture.
Why they work indoorsMost foliage houseplants originate from tropical forest understories — adapted to filtered light, steady warmth, and high humidity. Your home mimics that environment unevenly, room to room. Keep them away from heating vents, radiators, and the draft beside a frequently opened front door — dry, fluctuating air causes crispy brown leaf tips even when watering is correct.
Flowering Houseplants ▶
  • African Violet
  • Anthurium
  • Orchid (Phalaenopsis)
  • Bromeliad
  • Begonia
  • Spathiphyllum
  • Kalanchoe
  • Gardenia
Where to Put Them
🌅 Best: South- or West-Facing Sill
Flowering takes more energy than leaf growth, so bloomers need the brightest light in the house. Set pots within a few inches of an unobstructed south- or west-facing window; sheer curtains diffuse harsh midday sun for delicate bloomers like African Violet without dimming the room.
🌄 Good Substitute: East-Facing Window
Gentler morning light works for most flowering houseplants when south/west exposure isn't available. Orchids do well here, and rebloom faster with a 3–5°C night-time temperature drop — an unheated east-facing bedroom often supplies this naturally.
Light triggers floweringMost indoor bloomers need bright indirect light to rebloom. Think of light as the "ignition switch" — without enough of it, the plant stays vegetative. North-facing rooms rarely supply enough light for reblooming even with a window — move bloomers there only temporarily while in flower, then return them to a brighter sill.
Succulents & Cacti ▶
  • Aloe Vera
  • Echeveria
  • Haworthia
  • Jade Plant
  • Barrel Cactus
  • Christmas Cactus
  • Sedum
  • Gasteria
Where to Put Them
☀️ Best: Living Room / Sunroom, South Window
An unobstructed south-facing sill with stable room temperature is ideal — strong, consistent light without the humidity swings of a kitchen or bath. This is the single best spot in most homes for Echeveria, Jade, and barrel cacti.
🚫 Avoid: Bathrooms & Kitchens
The same humidity that foliage plants love causes succulents to stretch (etiolate) toward what little light is available and invites root and stem rot. Also keep them off radiators and away from frequently opened exterior doors — temperature swings stress their dormancy cycle.
Store water, not attentionSucculents thrive on neglect. They store water in their tissues the same way a camel stores it in fat reserves — built for drought, not abundance. Misting them is counterproductive; a dry room with strong light will always outperform a humid room with weak light.
Air-Purifying Plants ▶
  • Spider Plant
  • Boston Fern
  • Dracaena
  • Bamboo Palm
  • English Ivy
  • Areca Palm
Where to Put Them
🛏 Best: Bedroom
You spend 7–9 hours a night breathing whatever air is in this room. Snake Plant and Areca Palm suit average bedroom light and keep working quietly overnight.
🍳 Also Good: Kitchen
Combustion byproducts from gas stoves and aerosolized cooking compounds make the kitchen a high-value spot for VOC-active species. Bamboo Palm and Spider Plant tolerate the kitchen's higher humidity while still earning their keep on air quality.
NASA Clean Air StudyThese species were identified as effective VOC reducers in a 1989 NASA study on sealed-chamber air quality. NASA's own rule of thumb is roughly one plant per 100 sq ft of floor space — a single pot in a large room won't move the needle much. Newly renovated rooms off-gassing paint, adhesive, or new flooring benefit most.
Low-Light Tolerant ▶
  • Cast Iron Plant
  • Pothos (Golden)
  • ZZ Plant
  • Peace Lily
  • Chinese Evergreen
  • Parlour Palm
Where to Put Them
🚪 Best: Interior Hallways & North-Facing Rooms
These are the "problem corners" of most homes — windowless hallways, north-facing guest rooms, stairwells. ZZ Plant and Cast Iron Plant are genuinely built for neglect and will outlast almost anything else placed there.
🖥 Also Good: Windowless Office / Cubicle
Snake Plant and Parlour Palm tolerate standard fluorescent or LED office lighting as their only light source. If the room has zero natural light, run a basic grow light or leave overhead lights on roughly 12 hours a day — light, even artificial, is still required.
Dim rooms, not dark rooms"Low light" still means some daylight — think north-facing window or a bright hallway near a skylight. True darkness, like an interior closet, won't sustain any plant regardless of species. These just need far less than a south-facing sill.
Pet & Child Safety ▶
  • Toxic: Pothos
  • Toxic: Peace Lily
  • Toxic: Monstera
  • Toxic: Dracaena
  • Safe: Spider Plant
  • Safe: Boston Fern
  • Safe: Areca Palm
  • Safe: Haworthia
Placement Strategy, Not Just Plant Choice
🪴 Safe Plants → Floor Level, Anywhere
Reserve low shelves, coffee tables, and floor-standing pots in family rooms and kids' play areas for plants on the "Safe" list — Spider Plant, Boston Fern, Areca Palm, Haworthia. No placement strategy needed because there's nothing to guard against.
⬆️ Toxic Plants → Elevated & Out of Reach
Most dogs can't reach the top of a tall shelf or a hanging bracket; most cats can — so in cat households favour a separate room with a door that stays closed (a sunroom or office) over a high shelf. Pothos, Peace Lily, Monstera, and Dracaena are common offenders worth relocating this way.
Always verify with ASPCA — and know the severity isn't uniformToxicity ranges widely: Pothos, Peace Lily, and Dracaena typically cause mouth irritation, drooling, and vomiting from calcium oxalate crystals — unpleasant but rarely dangerous. True lilies (Lilium species, not Peace Lily) are a different story: even small amounts can cause fatal kidney failure in cats. Always cross-check the exact species against the ASPCA Toxic Plant Database before placing it in a home with pets or young children, and keep ASPCA Animal Poison Control (888-426-4435) saved in your phone.
🌳 Outdoor Plants 6 topics ▶
Most people place outdoor plants by best guess too — whatever's left over goes wherever there's an empty patch of soil, with no regard for the long-term consequences. A property has the same kind of variation a home's interior does: a shaded, moisture-retaining north side; a hot, dry foundation bed baking in reflected heat; low spots that stay soggy after rain; fence lines that double as privacy screens. Match the plant to the actual site, not the nearest gap. Each topic below includes a Where to Plant Them section with specific placement guidance and companion species.
Perennials ▶
  • Black-Eyed Susan
  • Coneflower
  • Hostas
  • Day Lily
  • Russian Sage
  • Astilbe
  • Bleeding Heart
  • Salvia
Where to Plant Them
☀️ Best: South/East Foundation Border
Sun-lovers like Coneflower, Russian Sage, and Daylily thrive on the reflected heat off siding. Plant a full shrub-width away from the wall — close enough to read as part of the house, far enough that roots stay clear of the foundation and roof runoff doesn't splash leaves with grit.
🌑 Swap In: North-Facing Foundation
If your foundation bed sits on the shaded north side, the same "always something planted" effect comes from Hostas, Astilbe, and Bleeding Heart instead — they actually prefer the cooler, moister soil that collects there.
Pairs Well With
  • Boxwood (winter structure)
  • Ornamental Grasses (texture)
  • Catmint (drought-tolerant edge)
  • Spring Bulbs (early color)
Plant once, enjoy for yearsPerennials are like a long-term infrastructure investment — higher upfront cost than annuals, but they compound value season after season without replanting. Coneflower, Russian Sage, and ornamental grasses share the same drought tolerance and overlapping bloom window, which is why they read as an intentional grouping rather than a random assortment.
Annuals ▶
  • Marigolds
  • Petunias
  • Impatiens
  • Zinnias
  • Lobelia
  • Snapdragons
  • Cosmos
  • Nasturtiums
Where to Plant Them
🚪 Best: Entryways, Porch Containers & Borders
Annuals deliver maximum seasonal color exactly where it's seen most — a front-door urn, a porch rail box, the front edge of a foundation bed. Because the planting is temporary, this is also the lowest-risk place to experiment with a bold color combination.
🌱 Also Good: Gap-Filler in Young Beds
Tuck annuals into bare soil left by spring bulbs that have died back or perennials still filling in toward mature size. They bloom continuously through the gap years while the permanent planting establishes.
Pairs Well With
  • Coleus (shade foliage contrast)
  • Snapdragons (upright thriller)
  • Marigolds along veg beds (pest deterrence)
Full-season colour strategyAnnuals are the "quick wins" of garden design — they bloom hard all season, letting you refresh colour palette each year without commitment. Use the thriller/filler/spiller formula in containers: one upright plant for height, mid-height fillers around it, and a trailing spiller like Lobelia over the rim.
Shrubs & Hedges ▶
  • Boxwood
  • Lilac
  • Forsythia
  • Hydrangea
  • Rose of Sharon
  • Spirea
  • Viburnum
  • Holly
  • Roses

Where to Plant Them
🧱 Best: Property Line — Privacy Screening
Stagger evergreen (Holly) and deciduous (Lilac, Viburnum) shrubs in a zigzag rather than a single row — it fills in fuller and adds depth. Most municipalities require hedges to sit 3–6 ft back from the property line, so confirm the local setback before planting directly on it.
📍 Also Good: Foundation Anchor Points
House corners and either side of the front door read better with one substantial shrub (Viburnum, Hydrangea) than a scattered mix. Plant at least one full mature-width away from the wall so siding stays dry and roots stay clear of the foundation.
Pairs Well With
  • Spring Bulbs (bloom before leaf-out)
  • Russian Sage (continues color after shrub bloom)
  • Catmint (softens the base)
Structure before softnessShrubs are the "bones" of garden architecture. Design with shrubs first, then layer perennials and annuals around them — the same way an architect places walls before furniture. Underplant deciduous shrubs like Lilac and Forsythia with spring bulbs that bloom and fade before the shrub leafs out, so the same square foot of bed works twice.
Vegetables & Herbs ▶

Where to Plant Them
☀️ Best: Open, Level Ground — 6-8h Direct Sun
Keep the vegetable bed clear of tree and large shrub canopies — their roots compete for the same water and nutrients, and their shade cuts fruiting yield. Good air circulation across an open bed also reduces fungal disease pressure on tomatoes and squash.
🚪 Also Good: Herb Bed by the Kitchen Door
A small bed or containers of chives, basil, and cilantro right outside the kitchen door actually get used. Keep mint in a container even here — it's aggressively invasive in open ground and will colonize the bed within two seasons.
Pairs Well With
  • Basil beside Tomatoes (flavor + pest deterrence)
  • Chives as bed border (deters aphids, beetles)
  • Lettuce in Tomato's afternoon shade (delays bolting)

Native & Pollinator Plants ▶
  • Wild Bergamot
  • Milkweed
  • Goldenrod
  • Buttonbush
  • Joe Pye Weed
  • Ironweed
  • Purple Coneflower
  • Wild Columbine
Where to Plant Them
🌾 Best: Yard Edges & Naturalized Margins
Fence lines, the strip between lawn and tree line, or any area you're not mowing weekly. Plant in drifts of three or more of the same species rather than single specimens — that's what actually gets pollinators to find and use the patch.
💧 Also Good: Low, Damp Corners
Buttonbush and Joe Pye Weed are two of the few showy natives that want consistently moist soil. Use them in a low spot that stays soggy after rain instead of fighting the drainage with amendments — it's the right plant for a spot most gardeners consider a problem.
Pairs Well With
  • Wild Columbine (spring bloom)
  • Bergamot + Coneflower (summer bloom)
  • Goldenrod + Ironweed (fall bloom)
Ecosystem returnNative plants are evolved with local insects and birds — they deliver biodiversity benefits that exotic ornamentals simply can't replicate, and they typically require far less water and maintenance once established. Stagger bloom times across the drift — spring Columbine into summer Bergamot and Coneflower into fall Goldenrod and Ironweed — so the bed feeds pollinators from May through October, not just for a few peak weeks.
Trees & Canopy ▶
  • Sugar Maple

  • Serviceberry
  • Magnolia
  • Crabapple
  • Eastern Redbud

  • White Spruce (conifer)

Where to Plant Them — Deciduous vs. Coniferous
☀️ Deciduous Shade: South & West Walls, 15–25 ft Out
Large shade trees (Sugar Maple, White Oak) belong 15–25 ft from south and west walls — close enough for the mature canopy to shade the roof and upper windows in summer, far enough that roots stay clear of the foundation. Bare winter branches still let low sun through.
🌲 Coniferous Windbreak: North/Northwest, 30–50 ft Out
Evergreens (White Spruce, Eastern White Cedar) planted upwind of prevailing winter wind on the north or northwest side block cold and cut heating costs. Keep them 30–50 ft out so drifting winter snow doesn't pile against the foundation — wind protection still extends 10–20x the windbreak's height downwind.
📍 Medium Trees: East Wall, 10–20 ft Out
Serviceberry, Eastern Redbud, and Crabapple are sized to sit closer than full-size shade trees — useful where a south/west spot is already taken or the lot is narrow.
🚫 Avoid: Within 10 ft of Foundation or Septic
If space is genuinely tight, choose small, less-aggressive-rooted species (Japanese Maple, Redbud, Serviceberry) that mature under 25 ft rather than crowding a full-size Oak or Maple against the house.
Pairs Well With
  • Spring Ephemerals (under deciduous canopy)
  • Epimedium / Hostas (root-zone shade)
Canopy is climateA mature tree provides the equivalent cooling of 10 room-sized air conditioners and can add 10–15% to property value. Right tree, right place — check mature spread before planting near structures. 💧 Indoor Plant Care Essentials 5 topics ▶
Care needs aren't generic — a Snake Plant and an African Violet fail for opposite reasons if you treat them the same. Each profile below covers the five essentials (watering, light, fertilizing, pests & disease, pruning & propagation) for one indoor plant type, plus specific products worth keeping on hand.
Foliage Houseplant Care ▶
Pothos, Monstera, Snake Plant, ZZ, Peace Lily, Chinese Evergreen, Rubber Tree, and Bird of Paradise all share a tropical-understory background, so their routines are more alike than different. Get these five right and most of this group thrives with minimal drama.
Watering
Let the top 2 inches of soil dry between waterings, then soak thoroughly until water runs from the drainage hole. Overwatering — not underwatering — kills more foliage plants than anything else; root rot from sitting in saucer water is the single most common cause of a "dying" Pothos or Peace Lily.
Light
Bright, indirect light — a few feet back from an east or south window, or directly in an unobstructed north window. Direct midday sun scorches leaves; deep shade slows growth and causes leggy, sparse stems reaching for light.
Fertilizing
Feed every 2–4 weeks during active growth (spring through early fall) with a balanced liquid fertilizer; skip feeding entirely in winter when growth naturally slows. Over-fertilizing causes brown, crispy leaf-tip burn from salt buildup — a far more common mistake than under-feeding.
Pests & Disease
Inspect monthly for mealybugs (white cottony clusters in leaf joints), spider mites (fine webbing, stippled leaves), and scale (flat brown bumps on stems). Indoors, these pests breed year-round with no winter die-off, so a 2-week quarantine for any new plant matters more than it would outdoors.
Pruning & Propagation
Pinch growing tips to encourage fuller, bushier growth; trim yellowed or damaged leaves at the base. Pothos, Monstera, and Rubber Tree all root readily from stem cuttings in water — a free way to multiply a thriving plant or rescue a leggy one.

Flowering Houseplant Care ▶
African Violet, Anthurium, Orchid, Bromeliad, Begonia, Spathiphyllum, Kalanchoe, and Gardenia all spend more energy on flowering than any foliage plant spends on leaves — their care routine has to fund that extra cost.
Watering
Most bloomers (African Violet, Begonia) resent wet foliage and crown rot — water from below or at the soil line, avoiding the leaves and center rosette. Orchids are the exception: they want a thorough soak then a total dry-out, mimicking rainfall-then-drought in their native tree-canopy habitat.
Light
Bright indirect light is non-negotiable — flowering is the most energy-expensive thing a plant does, and insufficient light is the #1 reason a houseplant won't rebloom. See the Flowering Houseplants placement guide under Indoor Plants for exact window orientation.
Fertilizing
Switch to a phosphorus-forward "bloom booster" formula (the middle NPK number higher than the others, e.g. 15-30-15) during active growth — phosphorus fuels flower and root development the way nitrogen fuels leaf growth.
Pests & Disease
Aphids and thrips target flower buds specifically, often before any damage shows on the leaves — check buds weekly, not just foliage. Botrytis (grey mold) attacks spent blooms in humid conditions; deadhead promptly rather than letting flowers collapse on the plant.
Pruning & Propagation
Deadhead spent blooms immediately — energy spent ripening a dead flower's seed is energy not spent on the next bud. For orchids, cut the flower spike (not the leaves) after blooms drop; most divide cleanly at the root ball every 2–3 years.

Light triggers floweringMost indoor bloomers need bright indirect light to rebloom — think of fertilizer as the budget and light as the permit. Without the permit (light), no amount of budget (fertilizer) gets the project (flowers) built.
Succulents & Cacti Care ▶
Aloe, Echeveria, Haworthia, Jade, Barrel Cactus, Christmas Cactus, Sedum, and Gasteria evolved in nutrient-poor, dry soil — nearly every care mistake with this group comes from treating them like a tropical foliage plant.
Watering
Soak thoroughly, then let the soil dry out completely before watering again — for most homes that's every 2–3 weeks, less in winter dormancy. Check by inserting a finger 2 inches deep; if there's any dampness at all, wait.
Light
The brightest direct light your home offers — an unobstructed south-facing window is ideal. Insufficient light causes etiolation: stems stretch and pale reaching for a light source, and the stretching is permanent — only new growth will look normal again.
Fertilizing
A diluted, low-nitrogen formula (the first NPK number lowest, e.g. 2-7-7) once or twice in spring/summer is enough. Standard houseplant fertilizer strength causes weak, distorted growth in plants built for nutrient-poor soil.
Pests & Disease
Mealybugs hide in the tight crevices between leaves on Echeveria and Haworthia rosettes — a cotton swab dipped in rubbing alcohol removes them directly. Root and stem rot from overwatering is the dominant disease risk here, not insects.
Pruning & Propagation
Most succulents propagate from a single leaf or offset ("pup") laid on dry soil and left alone until it roots — no rooting hormone needed. Remove shriveled lower leaves to improve airflow and reduce rot risk at the base.

Air-Purifying Plant Care ▶
Spider Plant, Boston Fern, Dracaena, Bamboo Palm, English Ivy, and Areca Palm are grouped by function (VOC removal), not by shared biology — so their care varies more within this topic than within the others.
Watering
Varies by species more than other groups: Boston Fern and Bamboo Palm want consistently moist (not soggy) soil, while Spider Plant and Dracaena prefer to dry out between waterings. Match the schedule to the individual plant, not the category.
Light
Medium to bright indirect light for most of this group; English Ivy and Areca Palm tolerate lower light than Dracaena, which yellows and drops leaves if kept too dim for too long.
Fertilizing
A balanced liquid fertilizer monthly during spring and summer is enough — these are foliage-focused growers, not heavy feeders. Skip fertilizing for the first 6 months after a new plant arrives; it's usually still running on pre-applied nursery feed.
Pests & Disease
Spider mites thrive in the dry air heating systems create in winter — exactly when this group is doing its hardest work filtering indoor air. Raising humidity around the plant (grouping, a pebble tray) reduces mite pressure as a side effect.
Pruning & Propagation
Trim brown fern fronds and yellowed palm leaves at the base — they don't green back up and just draw energy. Spider Plant is the easiest of this group to propagate: pot up the baby plantlets ("pups") it sends out on runners.

Low-Light Tolerant Care ▶
Cast Iron Plant, Golden Pothos, ZZ Plant, Peace Lily, Chinese Evergreen, and Parlour Palm are forgiving of bad light — the care risk with this group shifts almost entirely to overwatering instead.
Watering
These plants grow slowly in low light, which means they use water slowly too — overwatering is the single biggest risk in this group, not underwatering. Let soil dry out fully and wait an extra few days beyond what feels intuitive.
Light
Tolerant of low light doesn't mean thriving in it — even Cast Iron Plant and ZZ Plant grow visibly faster and fuller with a few hours of bright indirect light or a basic grow light. "Survives" and "thrives" are different bars.
Fertilizing
Feed sparingly — about half the frequency of a bright-light plant, since slower growth means slower nutrient use. A light feed every 6–8 weeks in growing season is typically enough; more risks fertilizer burn with no growth to show for it.
Pests & Disease
Fungus gnats are the dominant problem here — not because the plants are unhealthy, but because slow water use means soil stays damp longer, exactly what gnat larvae need. Let soil dry fully between waterings to break the breeding cycle.
Pruning & Propagation
Minimal pruning needed — remove yellowed leaves at the base as they appear. ZZ Plant and Snake Plant both divide cleanly at the rhizome or root ball if you want to multiply a mature plant rather than wait on slow new growth.

🌦 Outdoor Plant Care Essentials 7 topics ▶
Outdoor care runs on the calendar in a way indoor care doesn't — watering needs swing with rainfall, pests appear and disappear by season, and a pruning cut made at the wrong time of year can cost you an entire bloom cycle. Each profile below covers the five essentials for one outdoor plant type, plus the products worth keeping in the garage or shed. Roses get their own profile — they're disproportionately demanding compared to the other flowering shrubs.
Perennials Care ▶
Perennials return every year, so care compounds. Get the routine right once and it pays off for a decade; get it wrong and the same mistake repeats every spring.
Watering
Deep, infrequent watering (about 1 inch/week including rain) trains roots to grow deep; frequent shallow watering keeps roots near the surface where they dry out fastest in heat. New plantings need water 2–3x/week for the first month; established beds (year 2+) need supplemental water only during drought.
Light
Match light to the original placement decision — a sun perennial moved into shade won't bloom regardless of watering or feeding. The deficiency is light, not nutrients, and no product fixes that.
Fertilizing
One spring application of slow-release granular is usually enough for the whole season. Over-fertilizing perennials produces excess leafy growth at the expense of flowers and weakens stems — the floppy, top-heavy plants that need staking are often over-fed, not under-fed.
Pests & Disease
Outdoor pest pressure is seasonal, not constant — aphids and Japanese beetles peak early-to-mid summer, powdery mildew shows up in late-summer humidity. Treat what's actually present rather than spraying preventatively all season.
Pruning & Propagation
Cut back dead foliage in spring, not fall — leave winter structure standing for birds and visual interest. Deadhead through summer to extend bloom, and divide overcrowded clumps every 3–4 years in spring or fall.

Annuals Care ▶
Annuals live one season, so the goal is different from perennials — push continuous bloom for the whole growing window rather than invest in long-term root or structural development.
Watering
Containers and hanging baskets dry out far faster than in-ground beds — check daily in summer heat, sometimes twice. In-ground annual beds need about 1 inch of water per week.
Light
Match the original light requirement at planting — most popular annuals (Petunias, Zinnias, Marigolds) want full sun; Impatiens are the main shade exception.
Fertilizing
Annuals are heavy, continuous feeders because they're cramming a full lifecycle into one season — a weekly diluted liquid feed, or a slow-release granular at planting, keeps bloom output high all summer.
Pests & Disease
Aphids cluster on tender new growth; a strong water spray dislodges light infestations before a product is even needed. Powdery mildew on Zinnias spreads fast in crowded, humid plantings — space for airflow rather than packing tight.
Pruning & Propagation
Deadhead spent blooms continuously — the single highest-leverage task for annuals, more than watering or feeding. A spent flower trying to set seed actively signals the plant to stop producing new buds.

Shrubs & Hedges Care ▶
Shrubs are a multi-decade investment — most of the care that matters happens at planting and in the first 2 pruning cycles, not season to season after that.
Watering
Deep soak weekly for the first growing season while roots establish. After that, most established shrubs only need supplemental water during extended drought.
Light
Confirm the sun/shade match before planting — a shrub planted in the wrong light exposure will survive for years but never reach full size or bloom potential.
Fertilizing
Most established shrubs need fertilizer only once a year, in early spring before new growth starts. A slow-release granular worked into the soil at the drip line is enough.
Pests & Disease
Boxwood blight and leaf miner, lilac powdery mildew, and viburnum leaf beetle are the dominant seasonal issues. Inspect at bud break and again in midsummer — two visual checks a season catch most problems early.
Pruning & Propagation
Prune spring-blooming shrubs (Lilac, Forsythia, Weigela, Japanese Flowering Quince) right after they finish flowering — pruning in fall or winter removes next year's flower buds. Prune summer/fall-blooming shrubs (Rose of Sharon, Burning Bush) in late winter while dormant instead.

Rose Care ▶
Roses get their own profile because they're disproportionately demanding compared to other flowering shrubs — black spot, Japanese beetles, and winter dieback all hit roses harder than almost anything else in a typical yard.
Watering
Water at the base, never overhead — wet foliage left damp overnight is the single biggest driver of black spot. Roses want 1–2 inches of water weekly, deep and infrequent rather than daily sprinkling.
Light
A minimum of 6 hours of direct sun. Roses planted in part shade bloom less and are noticeably more prone to fungal disease because foliage stays damp longer without sun to dry it.
Fertilizing
Roses are heavy feeders — a dedicated rose fertilizer every 4–6 weeks through the growing season outperforms a generic all-purpose feed. Stop fertilizing 6–8 weeks before first frost so the plant hardens off instead of pushing tender new growth into winter.
Pests & Disease
Japanese beetles: hand-pick into soapy water in early morning when they're least active — beetle traps actually attract more beetles to the yard and shouldn't be placed near the roses you're protecting. Black spot: remove and dispose of (don't compost) fallen infected leaves promptly; the fungus overwinters in leaf litter and reinfects the same plant next spring.
Pruning & Propagation
Prune in early spring as buds swell, cutting at a 45° angle above an outward-facing bud to direct new growth away from the plant's center and improve airflow. Most roses propagate readily from softwood cuttings in early summer.

Vegetables & Herbs Care ▶
Vegetables are bred for yield, not resilience — they need more consistent inputs than almost anything else outdoors, but the payoff (an actual harvest) makes the extra attention worth it.
Watering
1–1.5 inches/week, consistently — irregular watering (drought then flood) directly causes blossom-end rot in tomatoes and split skins on carrots. A drip line or soaker hose delivers this far more reliably than hand watering.
Light
6–8 hours of direct sun for fruiting vegetables (tomatoes, peppers, squash); leafy greens and root vegetables tolerate more shade if full sun isn't available.
Fertilizing
Heavy feeders — tomatoes and squash benefit from a balanced feed at planting and a side-dressing of compost or fertilizer once fruit sets. Too much nitrogen late in the season produces lush leaves and delayed, reduced fruiting.
Pests & Disease
Outdoor vegetable pests are highly seasonal: flea beetles and cutworms in spring, tomato hornworms and Japanese beetles by midsummer, squash vine borer in early summer before larvae bore into stems. Floating row covers placed before pests arrive prevent more damage than any spray applied after an infestation is visible.
Pruning & Propagation
Pinch tomato suckers (the shoots between main stem and branches) to direct energy into fruit rather than excess foliage. Harvest herbs like basil and mint regularly — frequent cutting is itself a form of pruning that keeps the plant bushy instead of leggy and flowering early.

Native & Pollinator Plant Care ▶
The entire point of natives is that they need less intervention than the rest of the yard — this is the lightest-touch profile in this section by design.
Watering
Established natives (after year 1) typically need no supplemental water beyond normal rainfall — that drought tolerance is the main reason to plant them. The only watering that matters is the first growing season, while roots are still establishing.
Light
Match the species to the actual site condition rather than fighting it — a sun-loving Coneflower in shade and a shade-loving woodland native in full sun both fail regardless of any other care.
Fertilizing
Skip it. Native plants evolved in the existing soil fertility of their region; fertilizing them encourages weak, floppy growth and can favor weeds over the native planting in the same bed.
Pests & Disease
Some leaf damage from native insects is the goal, not a problem — native plants are food for native caterpillars and beetles, and a perfectly pest-free native bed usually means it isn't doing its ecological job. Intervene only if a planting is being defoliated to the point of dying, which is rare.
Pruning & Propagation
Leave seedheads and stems standing through winter; cut back in early spring instead. Many natives like Coneflower and Black-Eyed Susan self-seed readily — thin volunteers rather than treating them as a problem.

Trees & Canopy Care ▶
Tree care mistakes take years to show and years more to fix, which is exactly why most people get it wrong — the consequences are too delayed to connect back to the cause.
Watering
New trees need deep watering 1–2x/week through their first two growing seasons — a slow trickle for an hour reaches deeper roots far better than a quick surface soak. Mature trees (3+ years) rarely need supplemental water except in extended drought.
Light
Confirm the mature light requirement, not just the sapling's — a tree planted in too much shade for its species grows slowly and leans toward available light for decades, never developing a strong, even canopy.
Fertilizing
Most established trees in reasonable soil don't need annual fertilizing at all. A soil test is a better first step than a default feeding schedule — over-fertilizing trees pushes excess leaf growth at the expense of root and structural development.
Pests & Disease
Scale and aphid eggs overwinter on bark and hatch in spring, so dormant oil applied in late winter (before bud break) catches an entire season's population before it becomes active. A deciduous tree leafing out noticeably later than its neighbors each spring is the earliest visible sign of root or trunk stress.
Pruning & Propagation
Prune deciduous shade trees in late winter while dormant — wounds close faster before spring sap flow, and there's no leaf cover hiding structural problems. Never remove more than 25% of a tree's canopy in a single season; over-pruning triggers stress responses that weaken the tree for years.

Layout & Design Principles ▶
Most beginners treat an empty bed like a shopping cart — they fill it with whatever looks good at the garden centre that day. The result is a collection of plants with no relationship to each other. A designed bed is different: it has structure, scale, and intention. Here's how to build one.
The Three-Layer System
Layer 1 — Backbone
Tall shrubs or structural perennials at the back (or centre for island beds). These anchor the design and provide year-round form even when nothing is blooming.
  • Ornamental Grasses
  • Viburnum
  • Russian Sage
  • Joe Pye Weed
  • Spirea
  • Tall Sedum
Layer 2 — Body
Mid-height perennials and annuals. This is where most of the seasonal colour lives. Mix bloom times so something is always active.
  • Coneflower
  • Salvia
  • Coreopsis
  • Phlox
  • Astilbe
  • Day Lily
Layer 3 — Edge
Low-growing plants at the front that soften the border between bed and lawn or path. These tie everything together and prevent bare soil from showing.
  • Catmint
  • Creeping Thyme
  • Alyssum
  • Coral Bells
  • Dianthus
  • Lobularia
Core Design Rules
Plant in Odd Numbers
Groups of 3, 5, or 7 of the same plant read as intentional. Single plants scattered across a bed look accidental. Repeat the same plant in two or three spots to create visual rhythm — the eye moves through the bed instead of stopping at random points.
Create a Focal Point
Every bed needs one plant or element that draws the eye first — a large ornamental grass, a distinctive shrub, a birdbath. Without it, the bed has no hierarchy and nothing registers. Place it off-centre, not dead centre, for a more natural composition.
Leave Breathing Room
New plants look sparse. That's correct. Resist the urge to fill every gap immediately. Plants need space to reach their mature spread. A crowded bed in year one becomes an unmanageable tangle by year three. Use mulch to cover bare soil while plants establish.
Vary Texture & Form
Combine plants with different leaf shapes and textures: fine (grasses, ferns) beside bold (hostas, ligularia), spiky (iris) beside rounded (hydrangea). Beds planted with similar-looking foliage become a blur. Contrast is what makes individual plants readable.
Design the bed on paper firstSketch your bed to scale before buying a single plant. Mark sun direction, dimensions, and any fixed features. Then place your backbone plants, add the body layer, and fill edges last. This takes 30 minutes and prevents years of fixing mistakes. It's the same logic as drawing floor plans before framing walls — the cost of changing your mind on paper is zero.
Seasonal Succession ▶
The most common beginner mistake: a garden that looks great in June and is bare and dull by August. Seasonal succession is the practice of deliberately choosing plants whose bloom times overlap and hand off to each other across the growing season. The goal is continuous interest from snowmelt to frost.

Companion Planting ▶
Companion planting is strategic placement of plants that actively benefit each other — by deterring pests, attracting pollinators, fixing nitrogen, or providing shade. It's not folklore. Most combinations below have observable, repeatable results in home gardens. It does require knowing what to put where — and what to keep apart.
How It Works — Three Mechanisms
🛡
Pest Deterrence
Certain plants emit compounds that repel insects or mask the scent of vulnerable neighbours. Marigolds produce thiophenes that repel nematodes and aphids. Plant them as a border around your vegetable bed — not randomly — to create an effective barrier.
🌿
Nitrogen Fixing
Legumes (beans, peas, clover) host bacteria in their roots that pull nitrogen from the air and deposit it in the soil. Plant them beside heavy feeders like corn, squash, or brassicas. When the legume is cut at season end, the roots decompose and release that nitrogen into the soil.
🐝
Pollinator Attraction
Flowering herbs and natives planted near fruiting vegetables dramatically increase pollinator visits and therefore yield. Dill, fennel (kept isolated), borage, and phacelia are among the most effective. Tomatoes with poor pollination produce misshapen fruit — nearby flowers fix this.
Proven Pairings
PlantGood NeighboursWhy It Works

Plants to Separate
Fennel ↔ Most Vegetables
Fennel is allelopathic — it releases compounds that actively suppress the growth of tomatoes, peppers, beans, and brassicas. Plant it in its own isolated container or bed, away from edibles.
Onions ↔ Beans & Peas
Onion family plants stunt the growth of beans and peas. Keep them on opposite ends of the vegetable bed.
Mint ↔ Everything (in ground)
Mint is aggressively invasive. It will colonize a bed and crowd out neighbours within two seasons. Always grow mint in a container, even if that container is sunk into the ground.
Walnut Trees ↔ Vegetables
Black Walnut produces juglone, a chemical toxic to tomatoes, potatoes, and many other plants. Don't plant edibles within 15–20 metres of a Black Walnut's drip line.
Start with one proven pairing, not a full systemBeginners who try to redesign their entire vegetable bed around companion planting at once usually give up. Start with one pair — tomatoes and basil is the easiest. Observe. Add another pairing next season. Companion planting is a skill built through iteration, not a plan executed all at once.

Tools & Scheduling ▶
Most gardening mistakes aren't about plants — they're about timing. The right plant, planted two weeks too early or too late, performs poorly. Tools and scheduling convert a reactive approach ("I'll deal with it when I see a problem") into a proactive one where you're always one step ahead of the season.
Essential Tools — What You Actually Need
🪣 Garden Journal
A notebook or phone note where you record what you planted, where, when, and how it performed. Without this, you repeat the same mistakes every year. After three seasons, your journal becomes your most valuable gardening asset.
Start now: date, plant name, location, weather conditions at planting.
📅 Planting Calendar
A schedule anchored to your last and first frost dates. In Eastern Ontario (Zone 5b): last frost ~May 15–24, first fall frost ~Oct 1–15. Work backward from these dates to set every seed-starting and transplanting deadline.
Key dates: Start tomatoes/peppers indoors March 25–April 1. Transplant outdoors May 24+.
🌡 Soil Thermometer
Soil temperature is more important than air temperature for germination. Tomatoes need soil at 18°C+ to establish roots. Seeds sown in cold soil rot instead of germinating. A $15 thermometer eliminates guesswork entirely.
Check at 5cm depth before direct sowing warm-season crops.
⚗️ Soil pH Meter
Most plants perform best at pH 6.0–7.0. Nutrient uptake is governed by pH — even a well-fertilized soil delivers poor results if pH is wrong. Test once at the start of each season, especially in raised beds which drift acidic over time.
Test in spring before planting. Adjust with lime (raise) or sulfur (lower).
💧 Drip Irrigation Timer
Consistent watering is the single biggest factor in plant performance after sun. A basic timer-controlled drip system costs $50–80 and eliminates the most common cause of failure: irregular watering. It also frees you from the garden during hot spells or travel.
Set for early morning delivery — reduces fungal disease and evaporation loss.
🧊 Row Covers / Frost Cloth
Lightweight fabric that protects plants from late spring frosts and extends the fall season by 3–4 weeks. In Eastern Ontario, a late May frost after transplanting is common. Having row covers on hand prevents a single cold night from wiping out weeks of seedling work.
Keep on hand May 1–June 1 and Sept 15–Oct 15. Set a phone reminder.
The Eastern Ontario Monthly Task Schedule
MonthKey TasksWhat Happens If You Miss It
March Start tomatoes, peppers, eggplant indoors under lights. Order seeds. You'll be buying starts at double the cost in May with no variety choice.
April Start herbs, brassicas (broccoli, kale, cabbage) indoors. Apply pre-emergent mulch to beds. Transplant window shrinks; weeds establish in unmulched beds before you're ready.
May Harden off seedlings outdoors daily. Plant cold-tolerant crops (lettuce, peas, carrots) directly after May 1. Transplant everything else after May 24. Seedlings transplanted without hardening off suffer transplant shock and set back 2–3 weeks.
June Mulch all beds to 3". Stake tall perennials before they lean. Set up irrigation if not done. Plant succession lettuce. Unmulched beds lose moisture rapidly; tall plants once fallen rarely recover their form.
July Deadhead perennials and annuals weekly. Cut back leggy plants by 1/3 for a second flush. Monitor for pests and disease daily in heat. Spent flowers signal plants to stop producing; pests detected late are exponentially harder to control.
August Plant fall perennials (asters, sedum). Sow a final round of fast crops (lettuce, radish, spinach). Order spring bulbs. Missed fall planting = empty September and October garden; bulbs not ordered sell out by September.
September Plant spring bulbs. Divide overcrowded perennials. Apply compost to all beds before ground freezes. Overcrowded perennials decline in vigour each year; un-amended soil starts next season depleted.
October Leave seedheads standing. Cut back diseased material only. Apply 3–4" mulch around perennials after first frost to insulate roots. Cutting everything down removes winter wildlife habitat and next spring's structural interest.
Schedule is the difference between a garden and a collection of plantsA planting calendar is to a garden what a project schedule is to a construction project. Without it, you're reactive — responding to problems after they've already cost you the season. With it, you're always working ahead of the next deadline. A printed calendar on the fridge, checked monthly, is worth more than any single tool you can buy.

Soil Types ▶

Jar testFill a jar ⅔ with your soil and water. Shake and let settle for 24h. Sand falls first, silt next, clay last. The layer depths reveal your soil type — simple, free, actionable.
Amendments & Compost ▶
  • Compost (universal)
  • Perlite (drainage)
  • Vermiculite (moisture retention)
  • Biochar
  • Lime (raise pH)
  • Sulfur (lower pH)
  • Bone Meal
  • Blood Meal
Soil is the bank accountEvery harvest, every season, makes a withdrawal from your soil. Amendments are deposits. A healthy garden requires a positive long-term balance — not just chasing the current season's output.
pH & Nutrient Availability ▶
  • Ideal range: 6.0–7.0
  • Acid lovers: blueberry, azalea
  • Alkaline tolerant: lavender
  • pH affects iron uptake
  • Test annually
  • Raised beds drift acidic
pH is the master keyEven if all nutrients are present in the soil, wrong pH locks them away from the plant's roots — like a vending machine full of food with no working coin slot. Correct pH first, fertilize second.

The underground internetMycorrhizal networks connect root systems across a garden, sharing water and nutrients between plants. Healthy soil biology is a long-term investment in resilience — synthetic fertilizers can short-circuit it the same way antibiotics disrupt gut flora.
