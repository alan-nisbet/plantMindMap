🔍 Plant Selection Guide 8 topics ▶
Full Sun — 6+ Hours Direct Light ▶
☀️ South-Facing
Strongest, longest sun exposure. Heat accumulates along south-facing walls and beds. In Eastern Ontario this is the prime real estate for heat-loving plants.
  • Lavender
  • Coneflower
  • Black-Eyed Susan
  • Russian Sage
  • Tomatoes
  • Rosemary
  • Ornamental Grasses
  • Sedum 'Autumn Joy'
🌅 West-Facing
Afternoon sun — intense and hot, especially in summer. Plants here face the harshest heat of the day. Choose drought-tolerant, heat-resilient species.
  • Coreopsis
  • Yarrow
  • Gaillardia
  • Salvia
  • Sunflowers
  • Day Lily
  • Agastache
  • Catmint
Sun is a spectrum, not a switchFull sun means at least 6 unobstructed hours — but south-facing full sun is more intense than east-facing full sun. Think of it like the difference between a spotlight and a floodlight: same total light, different intensity and heat load.
Part Sun / Part Shade — 3–6 Hours ▶
🌄 East-Facing
Morning sun, afternoon shade. Gentlest of the sun exposures — bright but cool. Ideal for plants that need light without summer heat stress. Excellent for flowering shrubs.
  • Astilbe
  • Bleeding Heart
  • Coral Bells (Heuchera)
  • Impatiens
  • Ferns
  • Ligularia
  • Japanese Anemone
  • Hydrangea (bigleaf)
🌤 West-Facing (Part)
Morning shade, afternoon sun. Warmer than east-facing, good for sun-tolerant plants that also appreciate a shaded start. Watch for drought stress in mid-summer.
  • Coneflower
  • Rudbeckia
  • Phlox
  • Monarda (Bee Balm)
  • Foxglove
  • Spirea
  • Daylily
  • Veronicastrum
Part sun vs part shade — the distinction matters"Part sun" means the plant wants as much of those 3–6 hours as possible. "Part shade" means it can tolerate up to that much but actually prefers less. A plant labelled part shade in afternoon west sun will scorch — the label is a range, not a flat specification.
Full Shade — Fewer than 3 Hours ▶
🌑 North-Facing
Least light. No direct sun for most of the year in Northern climates. Often combined with cooler, moister conditions. Limited palette but reliable once established.
  • Hostas
  • Lungwort (Pulmonaria)
  • Sweet Woodruff
  • Dead Nettle (Lamium)
  • Tiarella (Foamflower)
  • Wild Ginger
  • Lily of the Valley
  • Solomon's Seal
🌳 Under Canopy / Dense Shade
Beneath mature deciduous or evergreen trees. Root competition, dry shade, and low light combine — among the most challenging garden conditions. Select woodland-adapted natives.
  • Epimedium
  • Pachysandra
  • Bunchberry
  • Wild Columbine
  • Jack-in-the-Pulpit
  • Trillium
  • Ostrich Fern
  • Mayapple
Shade is not darkness"Full shade" still means ambient daylight — open sky overhead even without direct sun rays. True deep shade (enclosed spaces, north side under dense evergreens) is a separate, more challenging condition. Use a phone light sensor app to measure foot-candles before selecting plants for difficult spots.
Mature Vegetation & Canopy Shade ▶
Deciduous Canopy
Shade in summer, sun in spring and fall. Plants beneath get a critical spring "light window" before leaf-out — use spring ephemerals that complete their growth cycle early.
  • Spring window: 6–10 weeks
  • Virginia Bluebells
  • Bloodroot
  • Trout Lily
  • Spring Beauty
Evergreen Canopy (Pine / Spruce)
Year-round shade plus highly acidic, dry soil from needle drop. One of the hardest conditions. Limit plant selection severely and amend soil aggressively.
  • Soil pH: 4.5–5.5
  • Bunchberry
  • Bearberry
  • Creeping Juniper
  • Acid-tolerant Ferns
Root Competition Zone
Within the drip line of a mature tree, surface roots compete aggressively for water and nutrients. Raised beds on top of root zones (not excavated) are the most effective solution.
  • Avoid deep-rooted plants
  • Use raised bed (4–6" min)
  • Epimedium (drought-tolerant)
  • Hostas (shallow-rooted)
Dappled / Woodland Edge
Shifting patches of sun through a loose canopy — the most forgiving and plant-rich shade condition. Mimics the natural habitat of the widest range of shade perennials.
  • Astilbe
  • Coral Bells
  • Ferns (mixed)
  • Lungwort
  • Tiarella
  • Native Sedges
Canopy shade is dynamic, not fixedA newly planted tree imposes progressively more shade each decade. A 20-year-old Sugar Maple that once allowed a sunny perennial bed now creates deep shade. Plan for the tree's mature canopy spread — approximately 2/3 of its mature height — not its current size. This is the garden equivalent of designing for a building's full occupancy load, not day-one use.

📐 Garden Planning 5 topics ▶
Layout & Design Principles ▶
Most beginners treat an empty bed like a shopping cart — they fill it with whatever looks good at the garden centre that day. The result is a collection of plants with no relationship to each other. A designed bed is different: it has structure, scale, and intention. Here's how to build one.
The Three-Layer System
Layer 1 — Backbone
Tall shrubs or structural perennials at the back (or centre for island beds). These anchor the design and provide year-round form even when nothing is blooming.
  • Ornamental Grasses
  • Viburnum
  • Russian Sage
  • Joe Pye Weed
  • Spirea
  • Tall Sedum
Layer 2 — Body
Mid-height perennials and annuals. This is where most of the seasonal colour lives. Mix bloom times so something is always active.
  • Coneflower
  • Salvia
  • Coreopsis
  • Phlox
  • Astilbe
  • Day Lily
Layer 3 — Edge
Low-growing plants at the front that soften the border between bed and lawn or path. These tie everything together and prevent bare soil from showing.
  • Catmint
  • Creeping Thyme
  • Alyssum
  • Coral Bells
  • Dianthus
  • Lobularia
Core Design Rules
Plant in Odd Numbers
Groups of 3, 5, or 7 of the same plant read as intentional. Single plants scattered across a bed look accidental. Repeat the same plant in two or three spots to create visual rhythm — the eye moves through the bed instead of stopping at random points.
Create a Focal Point
Every bed needs one plant or element that draws the eye first — a large ornamental grass, a distinctive shrub, a birdbath. Without it, the bed has no hierarchy and nothing registers. Place it off-centre, not dead centre, for a more natural composition.
Leave Breathing Room
New plants look sparse. That's correct. Resist the urge to fill every gap immediately. Plants need space to reach their mature spread. A crowded bed in year one becomes an unmanageable tangle by year three. Use mulch to cover bare soil while plants establish.
Vary Texture & Form
Combine plants with different leaf shapes and textures: fine (grasses, ferns) beside bold (hostas, ligularia), spiky (iris) beside rounded (hydrangea). Beds planted with similar-looking foliage become a blur. Contrast is what makes individual plants readable.
Design the bed on paper firstSketch your bed to scale before buying a single plant. Mark sun direction, dimensions, and any fixed features. Then place your backbone plants, add the body layer, and fill edges last. This takes 30 minutes and prevents years of fixing mistakes. It's the same logic as drawing floor plans before framing walls — the cost of changing your mind on paper is zero.
Seasonal Succession ▶
The most common beginner mistake: a garden that looks great in June and is bare and dull by August. Seasonal succession is the practice of deliberately choosing plants whose bloom times overlap and hand off to each other across the growing season. The goal is continuous interest from snowmelt to frost.

  • Holly (berries)
  • Boxwood
  • Evergreen Groundcovers

Map your bloom calendar before buying plantsList every plant you're considering and write its bloom month beside it. If everything blooms in June and July, you have a two-month garden. A well-planned bed has something happening in every month from April through October. Think of it like a restaurant menu — you want offerings across every course and season, not just one spectacular entrée.
Companion Planting ▶
Companion planting is strategic placement of plants that actively benefit each other — by deterring pests, attracting pollinators, fixing nitrogen, or providing shade. It's not folklore. Most combinations below have observable, repeatable results in home gardens. It does require knowing what to put where — and what to keep apart.
How It Works — Three Mechanisms
🛡
Pest Deterrence
Certain plants emit compounds that repel insects or mask the scent of vulnerable neighbours. Marigolds produce thiophenes that repel nematodes and aphids. Plant them as a border around your vegetable bed — not randomly — to create an effective barrier.
🌿
Nitrogen Fixing
Legumes (beans, peas, clover) host bacteria in their roots that pull nitrogen from the air and deposit it in the soil. Plant them beside heavy feeders like corn, squash, or brassicas. When the legume is cut at season end, the roots decompose and release that nitrogen into the soil.
🐝
Pollinator Attraction
Flowering herbs and natives planted near fruiting vegetables dramatically increase pollinator visits and therefore yield. Dill, fennel (kept isolated), borage, and phacelia are among the most effective. Tomatoes with poor pollination produce misshapen fruit — nearby flowers fix this.
Proven Pairings
PlantGood NeighboursWhy It Works
Tomatoes Basil, Marigolds, Carrots, Borage Basil may repel thrips and aphids; borage attracts pollinators and repels tomato hornworm; marigolds deter whitefly
Beans Corn, Squash, Carrots, Cucumber Fix nitrogen that feeds corn; squash leaves shade soil reducing moisture loss; classic Three Sisters system
Brassicas Dill, Sage, Rosemary, Nasturtium Dill and sage repel cabbage moths; nasturtium acts as a trap crop drawing aphids away from brassicas
Carrots Leeks, Onions, Rosemary, Sage Leeks repel carrot fly; onions and herbs mask carrot scent from pests

Plants to Separate
Fennel ↔ Most Vegetables
Fennel is allelopathic — it releases compounds that actively suppress the growth of tomatoes, peppers, beans, and brassicas. Plant it in its own isolated container or bed, away from edibles.
Onions ↔ Beans & Peas
Onion family plants stunt the growth of beans and peas. Keep them on opposite ends of the vegetable bed.
Mint ↔ Everything (in ground)
Mint is aggressively invasive. It will colonize a bed and crowd out neighbours within two seasons. Always grow mint in a container, even if that container is sunk into the ground.
Walnut Trees ↔ Vegetables
Black Walnut produces juglone, a chemical toxic to tomatoes, potatoes, and many other plants. Don't plant edibles within 15–20 metres of a Black Walnut's drip line.
Start with one proven pairing, not a full systemBeginners who try to redesign their entire vegetable bed around companion planting at once usually give up. Start with one pair — tomatoes and basil is the easiest. Observe. Add another pairing next season. Companion planting is a skill built through iteration, not a plan executed all at once.

Tools & Scheduling ▶
Most gardening mistakes aren't about plants — they're about timing. The right plant, planted two weeks too early or too late, performs poorly. Tools and scheduling convert a reactive approach ("I'll deal with it when I see a problem") into a proactive one where you're always one step ahead of the season.
Essential Tools — What You Actually Need
🪣 Garden Journal
A notebook or phone note where you record what you planted, where, when, and how it performed. Without this, you repeat the same mistakes every year. After three seasons, your journal becomes your most valuable gardening asset.
Start now: date, plant name, location, weather conditions at planting.
📅 Planting Calendar
A schedule anchored to your last and first frost dates. In Eastern Ontario (Zone 5b): last frost ~May 15–24, first fall frost ~Oct 1–15. Work backward from these dates to set every seed-starting and transplanting deadline.
Key dates: Start tomatoes/peppers indoors March 25–April 1. Transplant outdoors May 24+.
🌡 Soil Thermometer
Soil temperature is more important than air temperature for germination. Tomatoes need soil at 18°C+ to establish roots. Seeds sown in cold soil rot instead of germinating. A $15 thermometer eliminates guesswork entirely.
Check at 5cm depth before direct sowing warm-season crops.
⚗️ Soil pH Meter
Most plants perform best at pH 6.0–7.0. Nutrient uptake is governed by pH — even a well-fertilized soil delivers poor results if pH is wrong. Test once at the start of each season, especially in raised beds which drift acidic over time.
Test in spring before planting. Adjust with lime (raise) or sulfur (lower).
💧 Drip Irrigation Timer
Consistent watering is the single biggest factor in plant performance after sun. A basic timer-controlled drip system costs $50–80 and eliminates the most common cause of failure: irregular watering. It also frees you from the garden during hot spells or travel.
Set for early morning delivery — reduces fungal disease and evaporation loss.
🧊 Row Covers / Frost Cloth
Lightweight fabric that protects plants from late spring frosts and extends the fall season by 3–4 weeks. In Eastern Ontario, a late May frost after transplanting is common. Having row covers on hand prevents a single cold night from wiping out weeks of seedling work.
Keep on hand May 1–June 1 and Sept 15–Oct 15. Set a phone reminder.
The Eastern Ontario Monthly Task Schedule
MonthKey TasksWhat Happens If You Miss It
March Start tomatoes, peppers, eggplant indoors under lights. Order seeds. You'll be buying starts at double the cost in May with no variety choice.
April Start herbs, brassicas (broccoli, kale, cabbage) indoors. Apply pre-emergent mulch to beds. Transplant window shrinks; weeds establish in unmulched beds before you're ready.
May Harden off seedlings outdoors daily. Plant cold-tolerant crops (lettuce, peas, carrots) directly after May 1. Transplant everything else after May 24. Seedlings transplanted without hardening off suffer transplant shock and set back 2–3 weeks.
June Mulch all beds to 3". Stake tall perennials before they lean. Set up irrigation if not done. Plant succession lettuce. Unmulched beds lose moisture rapidly; tall plants once fallen rarely recover their form.
July Deadhead perennials and annuals weekly. Cut back leggy plants by 1/3 for a second flush. Monitor for pests and disease daily in heat. Spent flowers signal plants to stop producing; pests detected late are exponentially harder to control.
August Plant fall perennials (asters, sedum). Sow a final round of fast crops (lettuce, radish, spinach). Order spring bulbs. Missed fall planting = empty September and October garden; bulbs not ordered sell out by September.
September Plant spring bulbs. Divide overcrowded perennials. Apply compost to all beds before ground freezes. Overcrowded perennials decline in vigour each year; un-amended soil starts next season depleted.
October Leave seedheads standing. Cut back diseased material only. Apply 3–4" mulch around perennials after first frost to insulate roots. Cutting everything down removes winter wildlife habitat and next spring's structural interest.
Schedule is the difference between a garden and a collection of plantsA planting calendar is to a garden what a project schedule is to a construction project. Without it, you're reactive — responding to problems after they've already cost you the season. With it, you're always working ahead of the next deadline. A printed calendar on the fridge, checked monthly, is worth more than any single tool you can buy.

Jar testFill a jar ⅔ with your soil and water. Shake and let settle for 24h. Sand falls first, silt next, clay last. The layer depths reveal your soil type — simple, free, actionable.

Soil is the bank accountEvery harvest, every season, makes a withdrawal from your soil. Amendments are deposits. A healthy garden requires a positive long-term balance — not just chasing the current season's output.
pH & Nutrient Availability ▶
  • Ideal range: 6.0–7.0
  • Acid lovers: blueberry, azalea
  • Alkaline tolerant: lavender
  • pH affects iron uptake
  • Test annually
  • Raised beds drift acidic
pH is the master keyEven if all nutrients are present in the soil, wrong pH locks them away from the plant's roots — like a vending machine full of food with no working coin slot. Correct pH first, fertilize second.
